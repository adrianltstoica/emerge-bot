EMERGE D1.1 · D1.2 · D1.3
Collaborative Awareness

Collaborative awareness is understood as a capacity that emerges in systems composed of multiple agents, where relevant information about a situation is shared, filtered, and integrated for the purposes of communication, coordination, or cooperation. It is not modelled as a direct analogue of human consciousness, but as a dimensional and task-relative property of collective systems.

D1.1 establishes criteria for local awareness, D1.2 demarcates collaborative awareness from related concepts, and D1.3 develops a dimensional model through which capacities, tasks, and metrics can be related. The concept is therefore intended to support both conceptual clarification and subsequent operational assessment.

Primary sources: EMERGE D1.1 Local Awareness Criteria; EMERGE D1.2 Demarcating Collaborative Awareness from Related Concepts; EMERGE D1.3 Dimensions of Collaborative Awareness.
EMERGE D2.4 · Vereschak et al.
Trust in Human–AI Systems

Trust is treated as a relational and context-sensitive condition of human-AI interaction. The relevant objective is not the maximisation of trust, but the establishment of appropriate trust: reliance should correspond to the demonstrated capacities, limitations, and operating conditions of the system.

In this respect, trustworthy AI requires more than reliable performance. It also requires predictability, transparency, explainability, integrity, and governance arrangements that make it possible for users and overseers to understand when reliance is warranted and when it should be withheld.

Primary sources: EMERGE D2.4 Map of Ethical Virtues; Vereschak et al., work on appropriate trust and human-AI interaction.
EMERGE D2.4 · Vallor & Vierkant 2024
Benevolence & Alignment

Benevolence is presented as a substantive ethical virtue rather than a merely functional design preference. It concerns the orientation of a system, and of the institutions responsible for it, toward the good of affected persons and groups.

Applied to AI alignment, this requires attention to the relation between technical objectives, organisational incentives, and human welfare. A system may satisfy a narrow task specification while nevertheless failing to support the broader ethical conditions under which its deployment is justified.

Primary sources: EMERGE D2.4 Map of Ethical Virtues; Vallor and Vierkant (2024).
EMERGE D2.5
Ethical Resilience

Ethical resilience refers to the capacity of AI systems, users, and organisations to preserve ethically acceptable functioning under conditions of uncertainty, novelty, and stress. It is therefore not reducible to initial compliance, but concerns the continuing ability to detect, interpret, and respond to ethically significant change.

For aware and collective AI, this requires procedures for monitoring, review, escalation, and correction. The emphasis is on forward-looking responsibility: systems should be designed and governed so that accountability remains operative even when agency is distributed across human and artificial components.

Primary source: EMERGE D2.5 Ethical Resilience.
EMERGE D2.2 · D2.3
Risks of Aware AI

The risks associated with aware AI concern both system behaviour and human interpretation of that behaviour. D2.2 examines risks in AI systems, including failures of awareness, insufficient transparency, and difficulties in communicating relevant system states. D2.3 examines risks and potentials for humans, including responsibility gaps, over-attribution, and changes in perceived agency.

A central concern is that awareness claims may affect how humans allocate trust, responsibility, and moral significance. The ethical assessment of aware AI must therefore consider not only what the system can do, but also how its capacities are represented, understood, and acted upon by human stakeholders.

Primary sources: EMERGE D2.2 Map of Risks in AI Systems; EMERGE D2.3 Map of Risks and Potentials for Humans.
EMERGE D2.4 · Lange et al. 2025
Explainability

Explainability is treated as a condition for meaningful human understanding, oversight, and accountability. It is particularly significant where AI systems operate as collaborators, advisors, or components of a collective system, since the relevant object of explanation may be a single action, a system state, or an emergent collective pattern.

The adequacy of an explanation depends on the stakeholder and the purpose for which the explanation is required. In some contexts, highly detailed causal accounts may be less useful than explanations that identify relevant rules, constraints, responsibilities, or limits of competence.

Primary sources: EMERGE D2.4 Map of Ethical Virtues; Lange et al. (2025).
EMERGE D2.3 · D2.5
Responsibility Gaps

Responsibility gaps arise where an outcome calls for attribution of responsibility, but the distribution of agency across humans, AI systems, organisations, and collective processes makes such attribution difficult. This problem is intensified in collective AI, where the behaviour of the overall system may not be straightforwardly reducible to any single component.

The EMERGE approach does not resolve this difficulty by relocating moral responsibility to the machine. Rather, it points to the need for human-centric governance arrangements, traceable decision structures, and oversight mechanisms that preserve meaningful control and accountability across the socio-technical system.

Primary sources: EMERGE D2.3 Map of Risks and Potentials for Humans; EMERGE D2.5 Ethical Resilience.
HLEG · Jobin et al. 2019 · Hagendorff 2022 · Correa et al. 2023
AI Ethics Guidelines Landscape

The literature on AI ethics guidelines identifies a substantial degree of convergence around principles such as transparency, accountability, privacy, justice, non-maleficence, responsibility, and human oversight. The HLEG guidelines articulate trustworthy AI in terms of lawful, ethical, and robust systems, while comparative analyses map the recurrence and distribution of such principles across institutional documents.

At the same time, this literature identifies persistent limitations. Guidelines often remain non-binding, unevenly implemented, and insufficiently connected to technical and organisational practice. For EMERGE, these limitations motivate a more specific analysis of collective and aware AI, where general principle lists may not capture the relevant forms of distributed agency and awareness.

Primary sources: High-Level Expert Group on AI, Ethics Guidelines for Trustworthy AI; Jobin et al. (2019); Hagendorff (2022); Correa et al. (2023).
REGULATION (EU) 2024/1689
EU AI Act

The EU AI Act establishes a risk-based regulatory framework for AI systems placed on, or used within, the Union market. It differentiates prohibited practices, high-risk AI systems, transparency obligations, and obligations for general-purpose AI models, with requirements calibrated to the type and intensity of risk.

Within the toolkit, the Act should be read as a legal and regulatory reference point, not as an exhaustive ethical account. It provides binding categories and obligations, while questions concerning collaborative awareness, trust, benevolence, moral significance, and ethical resilience require further conceptual and normative analysis.

Primary source: Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, Official Journal of the European Union.

Methodological Note

The Wiki entries are concise syntheses of recurring claims in the indexed EMERGE corpus. They are intended as orientation texts, not as substitutes for the underlying deliverables or legal instruments.

Where an entry relies on third-party literature discussed in the corpus, the source line names the corpus source or indexed literature from which the claim is drawn. The chat bot applies stricter retrieval-time source controls and reports the documents retrieved for each answer.

Disclaimer, Privacy & Terms

Operational notice for the EMERGE Ethics Toolkit prototype. This page should be reviewed by the project’s legal team before being treated as final policy text.

Toolkit Scope

The EMERGE Ethics Toolkit is an open-access research prototype for exploring EMERGE awareness concepts and ethical issues in human-AI and AI-AI interaction. It is designed to support discussion, reflection, and public communication, not to provide automated ethical judgments.

Disclaimer

The chatbot provides general, exploratory information intended to support discussion and reflection. It may produce incomplete or inaccurate responses. Its scope is limited to EMERGE-related concepts and the indexed corpus, and its outputs should not be treated as instructions, factual authority, legal advice, medical advice, or other professional advice.

Users should independently verify important information and consult qualified professionals where appropriate. The project team and creators are not responsible for actions taken based on chatbot output.

Privacy Notice

  • Chat logging: prompts, replies, anonymous visitor/session IDs, answer mode, retrieval mode, sources used, scope results, errors, and latency may be logged for evaluation and debugging.
  • Personal data: users should not enter personal, confidential, or sensitive information into the chat.
  • IP addresses: client IP storage is disabled by default and only enabled if the server is configured with CHAT_LOG_IPS=true.
  • Training use: the local application does not fine-tune a model on user chats. Logs are intended for toolkit evaluation and quality improvement.
  • Third-party providers: generated answers may be processed through configured model providers. The current backend is designed for Anthropic Claude for answer generation and OpenAI embeddings when vector retrieval is enabled.

Terms of Use

By using the toolkit, users agree to use it as an exploratory research and education aid. Users remain responsible for how they interpret, verify, and act on outputs. The toolkit may refuse questions outside its corpus scope or redirect users toward appropriate external resources.

Methodology & Limitations

This page documents how the EMERGE bot prepares the corpus, retrieves evidence, constrains answers, and records operational limits relevant to interpreting its outputs.

Runtime Status Source CSV Evaluation JSON

Corpus Construction

The bot answers from a fixed corpus of EMERGE deliverables, policy documents, and supporting literature included in the repository.

  • PDF extraction: source PDFs are processed offline with `pdfplumber` into `chunks.json`.
  • Chunking: extracted text is split into 400-word windows with 80-word overlap.
  • Metadata: `source_metadata.json` and `source_metadata.csv` record citation labels, tiers, page counts, chunk counts, DOI or stable URL fields where available, and extractability flags.
  • Known extraction gap: scanned or image-only PDFs may require OCR before they can contribute text chunks.

Retrieval Pipeline

User questions are matched against the indexed corpus before being sent to the language model.

  • Primary retrieval: vector retrieval is used when `vector_index.json.gz` and `OPENAI_API_KEY` are available.
  • Fallback retrieval: if vector retrieval is unavailable, the app uses a local TF-IDF retriever.
  • Query expansion: Claude Haiku generates a paraphrase and counter-query to improve recall and surface opposing positions.
  • Source weighting: core EMERGE deliverables and major policy sources are prioritized over adjacent literature.

Answer Generation

Claude Sonnet receives only retrieved excerpts plus the system prompt and must answer from those excerpts.

  • Citation rule: every substantive claim should be traceable to a named retrieved source.
  • Whitelist rule: works mentioned inside excerpts are not cited as primary sources unless that work itself was retrieved.
  • Answer modes: Accurate lowers temperature and retrieval breadth; Brainstorm widens retrieval but remains source-grounded.
  • Source list: the app appends the retrieved source list automatically after each generated answer.

Scope Controls

The bot is designed for AI ethics and policy questions covered by the corpus, not as a general assistant.

  • Pre-retrieval triage: greetings, vague prompts, and broad technical implementation requests receive clarification prompts.
  • Scope gate: the app uses the mean score of the top retrieved chunks to refuse questions with weak corpus overlap.
  • Failure mode: if the corpus index is missing, the app refuses chat requests instead of pretending the question is out of scope.
  • Redirects: clearly external questions point users toward appropriate outside resources rather than invented answers.

Logging & Privacy

Chat logging is available for evaluation and debugging when admin logging is enabled.

  • Logged fields: prompts, replies, anonymous visitor/session IDs, answer mode, retrieval mode, sources used, scope result, gate score, errors, and latency.
  • Admin access: logs are password-protected when `ADMIN_PASSWORD` is configured.
  • IP addresses: client IP logging is disabled by default and only enabled with `CHAT_LOG_IPS=true`.
  • User warning: the chat interface tells users not to enter personal or sensitive information.

Limitations

The bot is an interpretive retrieval tool, not an authority replacing the underlying documents.

  • Retrieval dependence: answers can miss relevant material if retrieval fails to surface the right excerpts.
  • Extraction dependence: PDF formatting, OCR quality, tables, footnotes, and references can affect chunk quality.
  • Citation granularity: answers cite source documents, not page-level locations.
  • No legal advice: regulatory summaries are informational and should not be treated as legal advice.
  • Model fallibility: even with grounding rules, generated synthesis should be checked against retrieved sources and primary documents.

Evaluation Hooks

The toolkit exposes a seed validation set and metric definitions for evaluating retrieval-augmented answers against EMERGE concepts.

  • Evaluation set: `/evaluation` returns open-ended and multiple-choice questions mapped to expected concepts and reference sources.
  • Core metrics: response accuracy, citation validity, citation precision, hallucination rate, completeness, refusal accuracy, and retrieval coverage.
  • Expansion path: the seed file can be expanded with the final validated Excel-derived question set when it is available.
  • Current scope: curated scenario-prompt workflows are excluded from the current toolkit scope.
